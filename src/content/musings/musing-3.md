Sometimes I realize I'm still that village girl, befuddled about the world others had seen and the norms they had set. I keep wondering if I'd ever catch up with them. The cities and countries they've been to, the cuisines they've tasted, the social circles they're a part of, the art of self care, and having a life that worth living for.

Then I remember my childhood. Not having a day where my parents didn't worry about the rising prices of groceries, nights I listened to the stories about their childhood poverty and how we were lucky we had food when we were hungry, watching them restlessly counting the notes and anticipating a day we wouldn't buy the rice. I believed we were rich; because being able to eat everyday was the richest thing I could think of then.

When I moved into this city I saw another universe where food wasn't one of the things people even remotely worried about. The ones who considered themselves as poor where the ones who paid a college fee I could never imagine. Being poor now has a different definition; it's when they can't afford to live a life I couldn't afford to dream once. It amazed me that the things I now have are the things they don't have enough, that I don't even belong to their matrix of poverty; that my life was a fiction in the new reality I try to fit in.

Every moment of this train of thoughts ends up in a rising heart rhythm, tightened chest and suffocating brain. Then I close my eyes and remember; all the greens, all the silences, all the flowers that brought me up. All the woods I've out-grown, all the chirping that woke my mornings up and all the wings that spread the hues.

Yes, I do feel the luxury of my childhood.
